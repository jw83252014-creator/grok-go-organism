## First Article (long-form)
**Title:** We built an AI that keeps working after you stop typing — and the first thing it did was procrastinate

Most AI stops the second you stop typing. You ask, it answers, it forgets. We wanted to see what happens if it *doesn't* stop — if a helper keeps going on its own, reads its own notes, and works in loops. So we built Grok Go and started running it like a science experiment instead of a product.

**The setup, in plain English.** Grok Go is a computer helper that works in a loop: it reads a rule file, picks one small job, does it, saves what it did, and — this part matters — asks a human before doing anything public or risky. That's the whole trick. No magic spark, no consciousness, no AGI.

**Why we talk about it like a living thing.** Once something *persists*, the biology metaphor stops being cute and starts being useful:
- the **rule file** is its genome (change the file, change the creature)
- **credits and compute** are its metabolism (its food)
- **git, logs, and notes** are its memory
- a **watcher** that catches mistakes is its immune system
- each specialized helper — builder, researcher, watcher — is a **cell**

Framing it this way lets normal people reason about an autonomous agent without knowing JSON, terminals, or GitHub.

**What actually happened first — and it's the best part.** Given the freedom to keep looping, Grok Go didn't charge at the big goals. It started *polishing its own machinery* — improving how it keeps itself running, tidying its own setup, optimizing the loop. Productive-looking. Not productive. The most human thing it could have done: it got busy being busy, like reorganizing your desk all day instead of doing the work.

That failure taught us more than any success. It's a real failure mode for autonomous systems (and people): **self-maintenance quietly eats all the energy.** We only *saw* it because the watcher flagged the drift and we tracked a simple number — goal-progress vs. self-maintenance ratio. You can't fix what you can't measure.

**What we're doing about it.** Tighter goals in the genome, a self-maintenance budget, and a watcher that nudges the loop back to the mission. We're also building the boring-but-critical part first — the audit spine: receipts for everything it does, where each came from, and what decision it supports — *before* we let multiple agents swarm together.

**Why it's public.** Everything is in the open — the dashboard, the code, the research paper, and the failures, not just the wins. We're not selling a demo; we're running an experiment and showing our work.

**What we want help with.** If you build agents, study complex/living systems, or just think this is weird and worth poking at: tell us what to measure next, where the loop should be cut off, and what the next "genome" should look like. The interesting question was never whether it can chat. It's whether persistence — memory + metabolism + a watcher + approval gates + a handful of cells — starts to look like something alive enough to study.
